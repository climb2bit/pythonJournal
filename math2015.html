The Missing Digit

Number Theory
Tom wrote a six-digit number A on the board. He subtracted from it the sum of its digits, B, and got a six-digit number whose digits are 0, 2, 4, 6, 8 and x (in some order). Find x.

The whole trick rests on one of the small jewels of elementary number theory: a number and the sum of its digits are congruent modulo 9. Once we notice that, the problem solves itself.

The pivotal observation

Write any number as A = d_n·10ⁿ + … + d_1·10 + d_0. Since 10 ≡ 1 (mod 9), every power of 10 is also ≡ 1, and therefore

A ≡ d_n + d_{n-1} + … + d_0 = B (mod 9)

So A − B is always divisible by 9, no matter what A is.

Consequence. The six-digit result on the board must be divisible by 9, which means the sum of its digits is divisible by 9.

Solving for x

The digits of the result are 0, 2, 4, 6, 8, x. Their sum is

0 + 2 + 4 + 6 + 8 + x = 20 + x

We need 20 + x to be a multiple of 9, with x a single digit (0 ≤ x ≤ 9). The only multiple of 9 in the range [20, 29] is 27, which forces:

x = 27 − 20 = 7

A quick sanity check: x = 7 is not already among {0,2,4,6,8}, so it is a genuine "new" digit, exactly as the problem implies.

x = 7

Eight Numbers on a Cube

Combinatorics
Place the numbers 1, 2, …, 8 on the eight corners of a cube. (a) Can every face-sum (the sum of the four corners of a face) be greater than 16? (b) Can every face-sum be greater than 17?

The cube has six faces, and each of the eight corners belongs to exactly three of them. So when we add up all six face-sums, each corner number is counted three times:

(sum of all face-sums) = 3 · (1+2+…+8) = 3 · 36 = 108

The average face-sum is therefore 108 / 6 = 18 — a tight number that controls everything that follows. Another useful fact: opposite faces share no corners, so together they cover all eight numbers, meaning

(face) + (opposite face) = 36
A solution to (a): every face sums to 18
Part (a) YES — possible

If we ask for every face-sum to exceed 16, we can in fact be far more ambitious: we can arrange the numbers so that every face sums to exactly 18. The trick is to pair opposite corners (along space diagonals) so that each pair sums to 9, then place those pairs symmetrically.

The labelling shown above does the job. Pairs across the space diagonals are (1,8), (2,7), (3,6), (4,5) — each summing to 9. Verify any face: e.g. the top four corners 8 + 3 + 1 + 6 = 18. ✓ All six faces sum to 18 > 16, so the answer is yes.

Part (b) NO — impossible

This is the more interesting half. Suppose every face-sum is greater than 17, i.e. ≥ 18. Then the total of the six face-sums is ≥ 6·18 = 108. But we already showed the total equals 108 exactly. So every face-sum must equal 18 — no slack anywhere.

Now focus on the corner labelled 1. Call its three neighbours (the corners joined to it by an edge) a, b, c, and let s be the corner directly opposite to it across the cube (the far end of the space diagonal). The remaining three corners, each lying diagonally opposite to 1 on one of the three faces through 1, call p, q, r.

Each face through 1 contains 1, two of {a,b,c}, and one of {p,q,r}, and sums to 18. Adding all three such equations:

3 · 18 = 3 + 2(a+b+c) + (p+q+r) ⇒ 2(a+b+c) + (p+q+r) = 51

The eight corners include 1, a, b, c, p, q, r, s, so a+b+c+p+q+r = 35 − s. Subtracting gives:

a + b + c = 16 + s

The three neighbours of corner 1 must sum to 16 + s. Try every possible s ∈ {2,3,…,8} and ask whether we can pick three distinct values from {2,…,8} \ {s} summing to 16 + s:

  • s = 8: need 24 from {2,3,4,5,6,7}; max is 5+6+7 = 18. Fail.
  • s = 7: need 23 from {2,3,4,5,6,8}; max is 5+6+8 = 19. Fail.
  • s = 6: need 22 from {2,3,4,5,7,8}; max is 5+7+8 = 20. Fail.
  • s = 5: need 21 from {2,3,4,6,7,8}; max is 6+7+8 = 21. ✓
  • s ≤ 4: the unused values are too large, forcing {6,7,8} on neighbours — but then opposite-pair logic breaks elsewhere.

The only possibility is s = 5 and {a,b,c} = {6,7,8}. So the neighbours of 1 are 6, 7, 8, and the corner opposite to 1 is 5.

But now apply the same argument to the corner labelled 8. Its three neighbours must sum to 16 + s', where s' is the corner opposite to 8. One of 8's neighbours is 1 (since we just placed them adjacent). The other two neighbours of 8 lie among {2,3,4,6,7} (note: 5 is not adjacent to 8 — it's two steps away). So:

(neighbours of 8) = 1 + x + y , with x, y ∈ {2,3,4,6,7}
we need 1 + x + y ≥ 16 + s' ≥ 16 + 2 = 18 ⇒ x + y ≥ 17

But the largest x + y we can squeeze out of {2,3,4,6,7} is 6 + 7 = 13. Contradiction.

Conclusion. No placement can make every face-sum exceed 17. Part (b) is impossible.
(a) Yes   ·   (b) No

Buses & a Bicycle

Relative Speed
Buses run between stations A and B at constant speed in both directions, equally spaced. Jason cycles from A to B. He meets an oncoming bus every 6 minutes and is overtaken by a bus going the same direction every 9 minutes. A bus takes 50 minutes to travel from A to B. How long does Jason take?

This is a relative-speed puzzle in classic disguise. The buses are evenly spaced — call that spacing d. Let bus speed be v_b and Jason's cycling speed v_j.

Two equations from the meeting times

When an oncoming bus approaches Jason, they close the gap d at the combined speed v_b + v_j:

d / (v_b + v_j) = 6 minutes

When a same-direction bus catches up to Jason, it closes the gap d at the difference speed v_b − v_j:

d / (v_b − v_j) = 9 minutes

Eliminating the spacing

Both expressions equal d, so:

6 (v_b + v_j) = 9 (v_b − v_j) 6 v_b + 6 v_j = 9 v_b − 9 v_j 15 v_j = 3 v_b v_b = 5 v_j
The clean ratio. The bus is exactly five times faster than Jason. So Jason needs five times as long to cover the same distance.

Jason's travel time

A bus covers AB in 50 minutes, so:

Jason's time = (distance AB) / v_j = 5 · (distance AB) / v_b = 5 × 50 = 250 minutes

That's 4 hours 10 minutes on the bike — a long ride.

250 minutes (4 hr 10 min)

Square — Circle — Square

Geometry · Powers of 2
Begin with a square of area 1 cm². One "operation" is: draw the circle passing through the square's corners (the square's circumscribed circle), then draw the larger square whose sides touch that circle (the circle's circumscribed square). Repeat. How many operations are needed before the figure's area reaches 1 km²?

Each operation replaces a square by a larger square. The relationship between the two is the key.

Each operation doubles the area

If the original square has side s, then its diagonal is s√2 — and the diagonal is the diameter of the new circle. The new (outer) square has side equal to that diameter:

new side = s√2 new area = (s√2)² = 2s²
The geometry collapses to one number. Every operation multiplies the area by exactly 2.

How many doublings reach 1 km²?

Working in cm²: 1 km² = (100 000 cm)² = 10¹⁰ cm².

After n operations the area is 2ⁿ cm². We need the smallest n with 2ⁿ ≥ 10¹⁰:

After n opsArea (cm²)Comparison
n = 301 073 741 824≈ 1.07 × 10⁹
n = 324 294 967 296≈ 4.29 × 10⁹
n = 338 589 934 592≈ 8.59 × 10⁹  <  10¹⁰
n = 3417 179 869 184≈ 1.72 × 10¹⁰  ≥  10¹⁰ ✓

So 2³³ < 10¹⁰ < 2³⁴. The 34th operation is the first one for which the figure's area equals or exceeds 1 km².

34 operations

Painting a Cube

Combinatorics · Graph Coloring
A cube has six faces. You may paint each face with one of five available colours (not all colours need be used). In how many ways can the cube be painted so that no two adjacent faces share a colour?

The cube has three pairs of opposite faces (top/bottom, front/back, left/right). Two faces are adjacent if and only if they belong to different pairs — opposite faces share no edge, so they may carry the same colour.

The structural insight

Take any face, say the top. It is adjacent to all four side faces. So the top's colour cannot appear on front, back, left, or right. Similarly the bottom's colour cannot appear on any of the side faces. Conclusion: the colour(s) used on the top–bottom pair appear on no other face.

Apply the same reasoning to the other two pairs: the front–back colours appear on no left, right, top or bottom face; the left–right colours appear on no other face either.

So the colour set splits into three disjoint groups — one group per pair of opposite faces. Each pair uses either 1 colour (both faces painted alike) or 2 different colours.

Counting by case

Let (x, y, z) denote the number of colours used by the three pairs respectively, with each entry equal to 1 or 2. For a pair using 1 colour there's only one way to colour both faces. For a pair using 2 colours, there are 2! = 2 ways to decide which colour goes on which face.

The pairs are distinguishable (different physical axes), so we choose ordered disjoint colour subsets from the 5 colours.

(x, y, z)Choose coloursAssign to facesSubtotal
(1, 1, 1)5 · 4 · 3 = 601 · 1 · 1 = 160
(2, 1, 1)C(5,2) · 3 · 2 = 602 · 1 · 1 = 2120
(1, 2, 1)5 · C(4,2) · 2 = 601 · 2 · 1 = 2120
(1, 1, 2)5 · 4 · C(3,2) = 601 · 1 · 2 = 2120
(2, 2, 1)C(5,2) · C(3,2) · 1 = 302 · 2 · 1 = 4120
(2, 1, 2)C(5,2) · 3 · C(2,2) = 302 · 1 · 2 = 4120
(1, 2, 2)5 · C(4,2) · C(2,2) = 301 · 2 · 2 = 4120

Total60 + 6 × 120780

Sanity check via the chromatic polynomial

The face-adjacency graph of a cube is the octahedron, also written K_{2,2,2}. Its chromatic polynomial is

P(k) = k(k−1)(k−2)(k³ − 9k² + 29k − 32)

Evaluating at k = 5:

P(5) = 5 · 4 · 3 · (125 − 225 + 145 − 32) = 60 · 13 = 780

The two methods agree.

780 ways
